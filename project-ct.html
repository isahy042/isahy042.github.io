Project · Creacher Teacher

Learn the Ancient Art of Creating Color

Download Game (Google Drive) Github Link

The Creacher Teacher Magician Trade School is a world in which every student can get in touch with their inner artist and mage. Through the use of music, decorative objects, and a 3D wizard’s lair, one is transported into a unique Harry Potter-like land to learn the ancient art of creating color. Our goal is to make learning fun, foster creativity, and teach a spell or two. Upon beginning The Creacher Teacher Magician Trade School, users will be situated in a magician’s potion room with a cauldron and three flasks of liquid meant for mixing. In our attempt to teach color theory, users will also be presented with a color they will have to recreate. Users will have to drag a specific combination of their flasks over the cauldron to create the desired outcome color. This new color can be used to make different potions and is also immediately added to the achievement-based potion book on the top right corner of the screen. In order to beat the level one has to make all six possible potions, consequently completing the potion book.

As our game is specifically designed for educational purposes for an audience of children ages 4 - 12, simple controls, clear directions, and a non-consequence driven environment are imperative. In hopes of teaching how to play the game through experience, users’ three initial flasks are red, yellow, and blue, or the primary colors. The new color they attempt to recreate starts off easy by only requiring one of the flasks to be dragged over the cauldron. This way users can learn the main mechanic of our game without any text on the screen. Additionally, a white dot in the middle of the screen shows where they need to focus their attention, providing further directions on navigation and user controls in combination with the tutorial. As users get more comfortable with the game mechanic and user controls, the output color gets more difficult to achieve, sometimes requiring a combination of different flasks. It is important to note, however, if one messes up and the potion does not look like the desired color, an extra empty flask can be dragged over the cauldron to remove the contents via pouring the excess into the “trashcan”. Even the best magicians need an undo button.

Working in a team of 3, we were all involved in the design process and divided implementation and documentation according to each member’s speciality. As the only member with sufficient knowledge in Computer Science, I was mainly in charge of programming the gameplay, including the tutorial and UI.

The Creature Teacher Potion Mixing School vision was to create a 3D potion mixing game that is enchanting to look at, fun to play and informational at the same time without being too overwhelming at any point or too complicated. Our game is the intersection of the fantasy world and the inner artist, a fun and interactive potion mixing game to teach people color theory, aimed at the explorer and the achiever.

Our main target audience is children ages 4 - 12 seeking to learn more about color theory. However, there are no constraints on who can play the game, any age range or gender can appreciate the lessons from The Creacher Teacher Potion Mixing School, however we do expect it to be more engaging for younger people rather than older ones, especially in terms of game play time. We have aimed game play at the achiever and explorer. The aura of the game is reminiscent of Harry Potter style, with a spooky twist. The core experience of our game is color theory, mixing potions in a cauldron and creating a new potion to compare with the desired output, attempting to mix the correct colors to see what color they will make and achieve each color in the primary and second color library.

We found from playtesting on the available demographics (unable to test on our target audience due to covid constraints) that the game needed considerable tutorial and instruction because just being placed in a room did not convey to many players the purpose of the game. We also found that some players spent more time exploring the scene than they did using the main mechanic of potion mixing. To combat this we worked to make the game more tutorial oriented, with just using the red, blue and yellow color potions at first and adding AI directional elements.

Our research on other games for influence on the Creature Teacher Potion Magician Trade School led us to a few different avenues and inspirations. Little Alchemy was a 2D computer game that we looked into to gather information on successful teaching games that were both fun and educational. We wanted to create a 3D game with far less elements than little alchemy so that the game had one simple educational feature and also create a game that was fun to explore. Lego Harry Potter was a big influence on the theme and aura of our game. The mechanics of the game were looked at for comparison of potions and also scene set up. We believe that part of our target audience could either be current or future players of Lego Harry Potter if they find interest in our theme. We also connected with the idea of an educational game from Dino Dino from our Study Tour, we wanted to implement a similar learning element.

Play is integral to children’s cognitive and social development. In fact, researchers suggest that computer games make learning meaningful to students and is one way to meet students’ interests. While some teachers may be hesitant to introduce computer games into the classroom, there are inherent advantages that are difficult to ignore. For example, Knezek (1997) argues that computer games are instructional tools that strengthen school achievement (ranging in areas from algebra to grammar), cognitive abilities, motivation towards learning, and attention and concentration . Having a group of engaged children motivated to learn sounds like an elementary school teacher’s dream. However, there are components of educational computer games that also help out the teacher, such as providing precise and prompt feedback to students without emphasizing mistakes. Although integrating educational video games into school curriculum may sound as if students will be distracted from learning, it is in fact quite the opposite.

There is a reason why many children’s, young adult’s, and even adult’s favorite books revolve around fantasy worlds, and that is because these worlds are captivating. The Creacher Teacher Magician School offers this fantasy-world, educational type computer game that stimulates attention and concentration while not emphasizing mistakes. Art theory is not always taught in school, and if it is, art classes are usually seen as less important than other subjects. Our game not only exemplifies the importance of art and technology working together, but offers a different way to meet students' interests and galvanize excitement about art. Researchers argue that key design features of educational computer games are more effective in increasing student learning than others. For example, 1) adequate and adaptive feedback, 2) embedding repetition, rehearsal, and the drawing of inferences, and 3) animated graphics which increase achievement are all related to the strength of student learning. The Creacher Teacher Magician School relies heavily on these three components, evident though the 1) immediate change in color when a flask is added and deletion of color when potion is removed, 2) our main mechanic of dragging a flask, which users do over and over again, and 3) the prompt addition of potions to the potion book after successfully mixing the desired color showcasing achievements.

The game mechanic that we based our game design off of is the mixing of positions. This means creating the potion bottle prefab, figuring out a way to make liquid inside the bottle, being able to mix one liquid with another, and then the changing of one liquid into another color.

For the actual potion prefab we looked into making it have a physical liquid in it however it is incredibly difficult to implement the physics mechanics necessary in Unity to make a liquid possible, so we had to find another avenue due to time constraints (see potion screen). We created a prefab with a nested child to act as the ‘liquid’ which was just a sphere with a rigid body and a hinge to the potion bottle to give it the effect of rolling round, this gives the effect of a moving liquid without actual liquid physics. We wanted the object inside the flask to be more than a sphere so we used Blender to create a sphere that was a half circle. We needed to be careful with the pivot, for it should rotate around the center. When we exported the mesh as an FBX it included the camera controller so instead we exported it as an OBJ. Finally we used a hinge joint and two rigid bodies to create liquid visuals.

We devised a few different ways in which to approach our main mechanic, the first being pouring one liquid into another, however once we ruled out using actual liquids we also thought about dragging one potion onto another and having the colors merge. However this creates an issue where the color of potion is forever changed, meaning that if red was used to make orange, you could not use red again.

We quickly realized we would need a third party to mix the colors because the potion flasks were too small. Once the cauldron element was implemented we needed a way to incrementally mix colors. By this we mean if a player put a layer of red in the cauldron and then a layer of yellow on top of it when they meant to put a layer of blue instead we needed a way to backtrack that action (think of an undo button). We are very cognisant of needing quick fixes in a game targeted to an audience of children that is likely to make quick or rash actions. If the colors were automatically mixed that would have been very difficult and confusing to the player. Instead we limited the cauldron to only being able to have two color layers and then implemented a mixing function that was both functional and interactive. This way the player can not create colors we have not accounted for and if we wanted to increase the game difficulty in the future we could increase the number of layers allowed in the cauldron. Once the colors have been mixed in the cauldron, the player then has the opportunity to place that liquid in the empty flask and compare it to the desired output.

The way the potion is scored is it is compared to the color of the ideal function when it is placed in the range of the yellow circle. The colors are compared and then the index of the array holding the colors of the goal potion is incremented. We use static fields and methods to control the index of the desired color of the goal potion. We did this because there is only one potion that represents the goal so there does not need to be multiple color indexes.

We had an AI script but decided not to use it because we wanted potions to learn how to change colors after the student had completed the color combination but decided against this because it was too complicated for young users.

In terms of optimization we used tags to compare objects and we used GetComponent to find objects, part of using GetComponent was to use it as a generic. We also avoided searching the entire game for a variable for a game object by making it a serialized field which saved a lot of processing time, for example in the script FP control we made a serialized field variable for the game object mixing pot and since there is only one mixing pot we dragged it into the variable and created a reference for it instead of searching for it in the game each time.

For lighting, each chandelier had 8 light sources but we adjusted that to have only one in order to minimize the use of lights considering unity can only handle one light source hitting an object at a time. However, this created too dark of an atmosphere in our scene so we turned up the intensity of the light sources.

On top of this, we wanted to make the tutorial independent of the game itself, ensuring that the tutorial is an “add-on” and can be removed or disbaled at any time without affecting the game itself. Thus, we had a single script used to manage it. Within this script, we had many serialized fields of game objects allowing us to directly refer to them without going through a search process. We also used a series of booleans to keep track of the progress of the tutorial, disabling it after the entire tutorial was finished.

Our game appeals to those who play to explore new levels (“explorer”) and gain achievements (“achiever”) . The explorer can be excited by mixing new colors and trying different variations, as well as exploring the room. The achiever, however, can be excited by mixing each potion correctly and getting all the potions in the spell book.

We followed Jakob Nielsen’s 10 golden rules when designing the UI, including making sure all buttons are concise and consistent. Our game does look the other way at a few rules, because it is designed for children. For example, Jakob Nielsen’s 10th rule on help and directions says that a good game does not need much in the way of instructions however since we are aiming our game at a broad range of children it is the first and foremost priority to make sure the children understand how to play the game, therefore we have implemented some subtle game directions to help the user.

According to Tracy Fullerton’s Game Design Principles, our game does a successful job of avoiding redundancy and unnecessary information. There could be an argument made that some directions are unnecessary but for children under the age of 10 this is not the case and therefore our game is not unnecessary or redundant to our target audience.
